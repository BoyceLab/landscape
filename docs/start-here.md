# Start here

If you are living with epilepsy, or caring for a child with epilepsy, and no one has been able to tell you the cause yet, you are in the right place. Many people arrive here undiagnosed and new to all of this. Nothing here assumes you already know a gene, a diagnosis, or any of the language.

This page is a plain orientation. It is not medical advice and it cannot diagnose anything. What it can do is show you how a cause is sometimes found, how to connect with others even before that, and how to use this site.

## What this site is

A map. It shows genetic causes of epilepsy, the conditions they lead to, the organizations run by and for people with lived experience and caregivers, and how ready each of those communities is to support research. If a cause is found for you or your child, this map helps you find the people who share it. If a cause is not found yet, the map still helps you see the landscape and where support exists.

## Finding a cause

Many epilepsies have a genetic cause that can be identified, and finding it can change what is known about treatment, what to expect, and which community you can join. Whether genetic testing is right for you or your child is a conversation to have with your neurologist or a genetic counselor. A genetic counselor is a professional whose whole job is helping families understand testing and results.

A few starting points that help families navigate this. Links are draft until verified.

- **Epilepsy Foundation.** General support, education, and a 24/7 helpline for anyone affected by epilepsy, no diagnosis needed. *(verify link)*
- **Behind the Seizure.** A program offering no-cost genetic testing for young children with certain unprovoked seizures, ordered through a clinician. *(verify link and current eligibility)*
- **National Society of Genetic Counselors.** A "find a genetic counselor" directory to locate someone near you or by telehealth. *(verify link)*
- **Undiagnosed Diseases Network.** For people who remain without a diagnosis after a standard workup, a research program that takes a deeper look. *(verify link)*

These are starting points to discuss with your own clinician, not recommendations or endorsements.

## Connecting does not require a diagnosis

You do not have to wait for an answer to find others. General epilepsy support exists right now through the organizations above. And the rare epilepsy community, which much of this site is about, welcomes families who are still searching.

While you are here, open the [Atlas](atlas.md) and look around. You can browse the conditions and the organizations without knowing a gene. It is a way to see that none of this is as isolating as it can feel at the start.

## If a cause is found

If genetic testing identifies a gene, come back and search for it in the [Atlas](atlas.md). The reference view shows whether a foundation or registry already exists for that gene, with links to reach them. The network view shows the related conditions and organizations around it. Some genes have an active community ready to welcome you. Some do not have one yet, and knowing that is useful too, because it points to where new communities are forming.

## A note for caregivers

Caring for a child with epilepsy of unknown cause is exhausting, and the searching is its own weight. The support here is for you as well, not only for the person with the diagnosis. You do not have to become an expert in genetics to use this site or to find people who understand.
