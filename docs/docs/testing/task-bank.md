# Task bank

Realistic tasks to give testers during a session. Select 3–5 per session, weighted toward the tester's persona. A card marked *universal* is appropriate for any tester.

## How to use

During the session, present one task at a time. Ask the tester to read it aloud, then attempt it while thinking aloud. Don't interpret or rephrase the task unless they ask — and if they ask, repeat it verbatim. Their request for clarification is itself data.

Mark each task as **Success**, **Partial**, or **Failure** based on whether they reach the intended answer with a reasonably direct path. Note moments of hesitation, false paths, and any spontaneous commentary.

---

## Universal (any persona)

!!! example "Card U1"
    **Find the page's "last updated" date and the list of data sources. Tell me what they are.**

    *Tests whether provenance is discoverable. Watch where they look first.*

    **Success looks like:** They find both the date and the source list within ~60 seconds.

!!! example "Card U2"
    **Imagine you spotted an error on this site — say, a missing registry. How would you report it?**

    *Tests discoverability of the feedback affordance. Have them attempt a real test submission if they find it.*

    **Success looks like:** They locate the "Suggest a correction" mechanism and walk through what they would submit.

!!! example "Card U3"
    **Find a condition listed in the "Background" category. In your own words, what does that classification mean?**

    *Tests interpretation of the status taxonomy without consulting the legend first.*

    **Success looks like:** Their description matches the legend definition reasonably closely.

## Clinician

!!! example "Card C1"
    **You just made a diagnosis of a rare genetic condition in a child. The family wants resources. Pick any condition on this site and walk me through what you would point them toward.**

    *Tests whether the gene-to-resource path is short and trustworthy.*

    **Success looks like:** They navigate from a condition to at least one foundation or registry link and can articulate why they'd send the family there.

!!! example "Card C2"
    **Find a condition where there is an active therapeutic program but no registry yet. Is that possible to determine from this site?**

    *Tests filter/combination behavior and whether the structure supports clinical reasoning.*

    **Success looks like:** They use filters or scan to identify a match and can describe what they found.

!!! example "Card C3"
    **Pick a row that includes both a registry and a foundation link. Open both. Do they match what you'd expect, and would you trust them?**

    *Tests link integrity from a clinical eye. Watch for raised eyebrows.*

    **Success looks like:** Both links resolve to credible, relevant resources.

## Foundation / advocacy

!!! example "Card F1"
    **Find your own organization on this site (or the closest analog if yours isn't listed). Is everything correct?**

    *Tests data accuracy from the most informed possible eye. Take careful notes if they object.*

    **Success looks like:** They locate their organization. Any inaccuracies they raise become priority blockers.

!!! example "Card F2"
    **If you were trying to identify communities at a similar stage of maturity to yours — to learn from peers or look for partners — could you use this site to do that?**

    *Tests whether the landscape view enables the comparative analysis foundations actually want.*

    **Success looks like:** They can articulate a strategy using the site, even if they suggest improvements.

!!! example "Card F3"
    **Are the status categories — Established, Emerging, Background — fair? Would you classify your community where the site has classified it?**

    *Critical: this is where representation concerns surface. Listen carefully without defending.*

    **Success looks like:** They engage with the question. Disagreements are valuable data, not problems to resolve in the moment.

## Patient / family

!!! example "Card P1"
    **Imagine a parent has just been told their child has a genetic condition and they've come to this site for the first time. What would they see, and would it help them?**

    *Tests emotional and informational fit for a vulnerable audience. Plain language matters.*

    **Success looks like:** They give an honest assessment — including any framing or jargon issues that would block a real family.

!!! example "Card P2"
    **Find a condition that has both a patient foundation and an active registry. Imagine you wanted to join the registry. What would you do?**

    *Tests the path from this site to an actual community action.*

    **Success looks like:** They identify the registry link and describe how they would proceed.

!!! example "Card P3"
    **Read the description of [SPECIFIC CATEGORY OR FIELD ON THE SITE] aloud. Does it make sense to you? Are there any words you'd change?**

    *Tests plain-language fit. Family testers will catch jargon clinicians miss.*

    **Success looks like:** They flag any unclear terms. Their alternative phrasings are gold — collect them.

## Researcher

!!! example "Card R1"
    **Would you cite this resource in a grant application or paper? What would you need to feel confident doing so?**

    *Tests scholarly credibility. The answer reveals what's missing in attribution, methodology, or update transparency.*

    **Success looks like:** They give an honest yes / no / conditional with specific reasons.

!!! example "Card R2"
    **Identify three conditions where infrastructure (registry, foundation, or therapeutic) is partial. What would you want to know about the gaps?**

    *Tests whether the resource supports the landscape-analysis use case researchers actually have.*

    **Success looks like:** They find three conditions and articulate what additional context would make this resource useful for their work.

!!! example "Card R3"
    **How was this list curated? Where does the data come from? Are you satisfied with the sourcing?**

    *Tests transparency from a critical scholarly eye.*

    **Success looks like:** They locate the methodology / sources statement and give a substantive critique.
