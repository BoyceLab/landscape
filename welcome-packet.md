# Running a session

A practical guide for facilitating a 60–90 minute testing session. Use this alongside the [task bank](task-bank.md) and the [feedback form](feedback-form.md).

## Before the session

- Confirm the tester has the [welcome packet](welcome-packet.md), the video link (if remote), and a quiet 90-minute block.
- Test your own screen-recording and audio setup. Confirm recording consent is in place.
- Have ready: the site URL, the task cards for this tester's persona, observer notes sheet, feedback form.
- Close other tabs and notifications. Silence your phone.
- Have a backup plan for tech failure: a phone number, an alternative video link, the URL ready to share by chat.

## Session outline (75 min target)

| Time | Phase | What happens |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 0:00–0:08 | Welcome | Greet, confirm consent, set expectations, get the tester comfortable. |
| 0:08–0:12 | First impression | Show site for ≤ 30 seconds with no scrolling. Capture their unprompted reaction. |
| 0:12–0:20 | Orientation | Tester explores freely for 3 minutes. Then ask findability questions. |
| 0:20–0:30 | Categories | Ask the tester to interpret the classification system in their own words. |
| 0:30–0:55 | Tasks | Walk through 3–5 task cards. Stay quiet. Let them struggle a bit. |
| 0:55–1:05 | Cross-device | Have them repeat 1–2 tasks on phone or tablet. |
| 1:05–1:15 | Debrief | Open-ended questions. Capture overall impressions. |
| After session | Form | Tester completes the feedback form within 24 hours. |

## The script

### Welcome (8 min)

!!! quote "What to say"
    "Thanks for making the time. Quick housekeeping first.

    There are no right answers today. I'm testing the site, not you. If you get stuck or confused, that's a finding — please say so out loud.

    I'd love it if you could think aloud as you go — say what you're noticing, what you're trying to do, what's pulling your attention. Even if it feels obvious.

    I'll mostly stay quiet. If I'm not jumping in, it's because I want to see what you do on your own — not because something is wrong.

    This will take about 75 minutes. We can stop any time you need.

    Any questions before we start?"

**Consent.** If recording: *"I'd like to record this session so I can review later. The recording is just for our team, won't be shared publicly, and will be deleted within [X] weeks. Is that okay?"* Wait for verbal yes before starting the recording.

### First impression (4 min)

**Setup.** Share your screen with the site loaded but covered (a tab away or a blank document). When ready, switch to the site for 30 seconds.

Say: *"I'm going to show you a website for about 30 seconds. Don't scroll, don't click, just look. After 30 seconds I'll cover it again and ask you a few questions."*

[Show site for 30 seconds. Switch away.]

Ask in this order:

1. "In one sentence, what do you think this site is?"
2. "Who do you think it's for?"
3. "What stood out to you?"
4. "Was anything confusing or unclear?"

!!! warning "Don't correct"
    Don't correct them, even if they're wrong. Their first reading is data. Note their exact wording — it tells you whether your hero text is working.

### Orientation pass (8 min)

Say: *"Now please explore the site freely for about 3 minutes. Click around. Tell me what you're seeing and thinking as you go."*

[Watch silently. Resist the urge to explain or rescue. Note where they hover, what they click first, what they ignore.]

After 3 minutes, ask:

1. "Where would you go to filter what you're seeing?"
2. "Where would you find the legend or explanation of what these categories mean?"
3. "Where would you find out where this data comes from and when it was last updated?"
4. "If you spotted a mistake on this site, how would you tell someone about it?"

Time how long it takes them to find each. If they can't find one within 30 seconds, mark it as a finding and move on — don't show them.

### Category interpretation (10 min)

Say: *"I'm going to point you at the status categories. Without reading the legend yet, can you tell me what you think each one means?"*

[Let them answer for each category. Then say:] *"Now check the legend. Did your reading match? Were any surprising?"*

Probe gently if they breeze through: *"Is there anything about how these are defined that feels overlapping or unclear?"*

### Guided tasks (25 min)

Hand them task cards (3–5, selected for their persona — see the [task bank](task-bank.md)).

Say: *"For each card, I'd like you to try to complete the task while thinking aloud. There's no time pressure. If you can't do it, that's fine — just tell me when you've given up and why."*

!!! tip "Facilitator behavior during tasks"
    - Stay quiet. Count to five before saying anything when there's silence.
    - If asked "is this right?" — say "what do you think?" and let them decide.
    - If stuck for more than 2 minutes, ask "what would you try next?" Don't tell them the answer.
    - Mark each task: Success / Partial / Failure. Note the path taken and any hesitation.

### Cross-device check (10 min)

Say: *"Now I'd like you to pull out your phone (or tablet) and open the same site. I'll pick one or two of the tasks you just did and ask you to try them again."*

Pick tasks that exercised filters, sorts, or the feedback workflow. Note what breaks or becomes harder on the smaller screen.

### Debrief (10 min)

Open-ended questions, in this order:

1. "What worked well for you?"
2. "What got in your way?"
3. "If you could change one thing, what would it be?"
4. "Would you recommend this site to a colleague or community member? Why or why not?"
5. "Anything I didn't ask about that you want to mention?"

**Closing:** *"This was really helpful. I'll send you the short feedback form by email — please fill it out within 24 hours while it's fresh. Thank you for your time."*

## Facilitator anti-patterns

Common ways well-meaning facilitators contaminate their own data.

| Anti-pattern | What to do instead |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Explaining what a feature does when the tester is confused. | Stay quiet. Their confusion is the finding. If they're truly stuck after 2 minutes, ask "what would you try next?" |
| Apologizing for the site's flaws as they appear. | Stay neutral. "Tell me more about that" is your friend. Apology turns testers into therapists. |
| Leading questions: "Was that confusing?" | Open questions: "How did that feel?" or "Walk me through what you just did." |
| Correcting their wrong interpretations in real time. | Note them. Their wrong reading tells you the design is misleading. |
| Rushing past awkward silences. | Count to five. People often add their most useful thought after a pause. |
| Letting the tester drive the agenda. | Be warm but keep moving. You can return to a topic in debrief if it matters. |

## After the session

- Within 30 minutes, write up your impressions while they're fresh — even just bullet points. Memory degrades fast.
- Send the feedback form link with a short thank-you note (see [Outreach templates](outreach.md)).
- Transfer observation notes into the [synthesis template](synthesis.md).
- Flag any blocker-level findings to the team immediately — don't wait for the synthesis pass.

## Severity tagging

As you write up, tag each finding with severity. This makes [synthesis](synthesis.md) much faster.

| Tag | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| BLOCKER | Would prevent the tester (or their persona) from using the site. Must fix before release. |
| MAJOR | Causes confusion or extra effort but isn't a hard block. Should fix before release. |
| MINOR | Cosmetic, mild friction, or polish. Fix when time allows. |
| QUESTION | Raised an open question rather than a clear issue. Discuss with team. |
